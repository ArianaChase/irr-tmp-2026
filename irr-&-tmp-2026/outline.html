(Mechanics of space debris removal, p.24)
One significant disadvantage of using the robotic arm method for space debris removal is its inability to capture debris with high angular momentum. Currently, no space-flown robotic arm can effectively absorb the angular momentum of certain debris, which challenges the efficacy of this method. Additionally, attempting to capture debris with high angular momentum can result in high joint torques, potentially causing mechanical stress or system failure. This limitation indicates that, without prior altitude synchronization maneuvers or advancements in joint actuator technology, the robotic arm method may not be suitable for safely capturing all types of space debris [121].

This operation is needed to achieve robust capturing because both objects are rigid, and any misalignment of the contact between the chaser and debris can push the debris far away from the chaser’s rigid capturing mechanism, especially in case where the object is tumbling at high angular velocity (Wu et al., 2018). Therefore, the rigid capturing method is more applicable for cooperative targets that have a docking port/mechanism (Somov et al., 2019). ET-Class, p.10

Title Astroscale's space debris removal demo mission funded for 2026 launch
Citation https://www.space.com/astroscale-elsa-m-space-debris-removal-funding
https://www.astroscale.com/en/news/astroscales-elsa-d-finalizes-de-orbit-operations-marking-successful-mission-conclusion
Credibility Astroscale is a space debris removal company based in Tokyo, backed by ESA and UK Space Agency as clients. They are set to launch the world's first de-orbiting mission in 2026 to take down an uncontrolled satellite in orbit.
Summary ELSA-M is a mission in which a de-orbiter module will dock magnetically to a "prepared" satellite within a web of satellites owned by OneWeb internet services and drag it down to an altitude at which it will slowly de-orbit and re-enter the atmosphere, burning up, taking about 3 years to do so.

Feasibility: ELSA-M's mechanisms have been demonstrated to work at a smaller scale when its demo, ELSA-D, was launched in 2021 to simulate the collection of "space junk" that it brought up with it. Such a demo mission proved the capabilities of:
- Tracking objects from a great distance without navigation data from the client
- Rendezvous with uncontrolled object (keeping it in sight, moving to securely follow it)
- Autonomous correction, no ground-based human control in close proximity
- Usability of on-board sensors, cameras, thrusters
- Absolute navigation - Use of GPS and ground-based navigation in long proximity
- Handover of absolute navigation to relative navigation (autonomous tracking with on-board sensors)

ELSA-D's servicer will de-orbit naturally in 3.5 years, while its client will de-orbit in 5 years.
Relevance ELSA-M and ELSA-D are demonstrations of the feasibility of de-orbiter modules that actively drag debris down to a natural de-orbitable state. The demo mechanisms have been proven to work, and such a mission is backed by well-known agencies. Astroscale also emphasizes the need for standardized docking plates for all commercial launches, whether for internet companies or otherwise, to enable sustainable and simpler/less costly de-orbiting missions.
Quotes "The mission received 13.95 million euro ($15 million) through a contract with satellite Internet services provider Eutelsat OneWeb, funded by the European Space Agency and the U.K. Space Agency."

After docking with the selected satellite, it will drag its cargo down to about 220 miles (350 kilometers) where it will leave the satellite to naturally deorbit."
